About This Player

Historical context, archival provenance, and data notes for the October 1944 sortie dataset.

Historical context

This website follows the October 1944 combat sorties of the 12th Bombardment Group in the India-Burma theater, when American medium bombers based in India were striking Japanese transport networks and battlefield positions across Burma. The group’s missions targeted roads, rail links, bridges, supply depots, and troop concentrations, reflecting the central role of air power in a campaign fought across difficult terrain and fragile lines of communication. October 1944 was a pivotal point in the war in Burma: the Japanese drive into India had failed, the balance of the campaign had shifted, and Allied forces were moving from defensive survival toward reconquest. Rather than treating these missions as isolated entries in a chronology, this project maps them across time and space to show how repeated tactical sorties contributed to the wider effort to weaken Japanese control in Burma and support the reopening of land routes to China.

Source material

The October 1944 sortie dataset in this project is based on photographs taken during an in-person visit to the National Archives at College Park, Maryland. The working dataset was then normalized from those archival sortie forms into a local player-ready CSV and JavaScript file for this demo.

The archival catalog anchor for the material is the following National Archives record: catalog.archives.gov/id/596339

The historical country-boundary basemap used in the player is derived from a 1945 world boundaries GeoJSON dataset bundled with this project (world_1945.geojson), sourced from github.com/aourednik/historical-basemaps.

Data notes

This player is built from a manually reviewed extract table of October 1944 sortie reports photographed at the National Archives and transcribed into a structured CSV. Each record includes mission identifiers, squadron and base, primary and alternate targets, takeoff/bombing/landing times, route notes, observation points, bomb-load details, opposition, photo-aircraft notes, leaflet drops, and other operational remarks.

A local normalization script then parses those fields, converts written coordinates into decimal latitude/longitude, matches named places against a reference lookup, and generates both route geometry and event markers such as takeoff, target strike, salvo, breakoff, flak, and landing. The result is not a raw archival dump but a constructed research dataset: a player-ready map and timeline built from archival forms, transcription decisions, place matching, and selective inference where the original reports are incomplete or ambiguous.

Data limitations

This visualization is an interpretive research tool, not a literal replay captured from onboard navigation records. Times, target descriptions, route points, and event sequences come from handwritten or typed sortie documentation that sometimes contains gaps, ambiguous wording, or uneven levels of detail from one mission to the next.

Some coordinates and route segments are directly extracted from the source material, while others are inferred from base locations, target references, and intermediate clues in the reports so the missions can be mapped coherently. As a result, the player is best read as a historically grounded reconstruction of sortie activity for October 1944, with confidence varying mission by mission.
